## Proposed Changes

### Change 1: Feature accuracy -- "weekly import" language

**Why:** Feature Accuracy table (CIS) says the correct description is "weekly balance updates through manual import" -- NOT "auto-connect / bank sync." Line 63 says "It updates after each weekly import" which is close but should explicitly name it as manual import to reinforce the intentional friction that "The Weekly Ritual" article establishes.

> **Current (line 63):**
> Both partners can see the Safe-to-Spend number at any time. It updates after each weekly import. The hierarchy runs first -- giving, saving, bills -- and what remains is the shared spending pool.

> **Proposed:**
> Both partners can see the Safe-to-Spend number at any time. It updates each week when you import your transactions -- a manual step that takes ten minutes and turns financial awareness into a shared practice. The hierarchy runs first -- giving, saving, bills -- and what remains is the shared spending pool.

---

### Change 2: Missing Joseph Principle connection

**Why:** The updated CIS identifies the Joseph Principle as a key differentiator. In a piece about couples and financial peace, the "thinking in seasons" language is a natural fit -- couples who build margin together can weather lean seasons without the margin itself becoming a source of conflict. The "Deeper Truth" section (line 71) is the right place.

> **Current (lines 73-75):**
> The Bible has a word for this: stewardship. The idea that what you have isn't yours to consume -- it's yours to manage faithfully, together. The hierarchy isn't a budgeting trick. It's that conviction turned into a weekly practice.

> **Proposed:**
> The Bible has a word for this: stewardship. The idea that what you have isn't yours to consume -- it's yours to manage faithfully, together. The hierarchy isn't a budgeting trick. It's that conviction turned into a weekly practice. And when couples think in seasons -- building margin during plenty so they're prepared for lean -- the shared number becomes more than a spending guide. It becomes evidence that you're building something together, not just getting through the month.

---

### Change 3: Biblical attribution -- slightly more direct

**Why:** Voice Guidelines (CIS) say "Be attributive, not vague" and "Faith is stated directly." Line 73 says "The Bible has a word for this: stewardship" which is direct and good. But the connection could be slightly stronger -- the CIS wants "The Bible teaches..." patterns and credits God as the source.

> **Current (line 73):**
> The Bible has a word for this: stewardship. The idea that what you have isn't yours to consume -- it's yours to manage faithfully, together.

> **Proposed:**
> The Bible teaches that what you have isn't yours to consume -- it's yours to manage faithfully. Stewardship. And when two people share a life, that stewardship is a shared calling, not a solo project.

Note: This is a low-priority revision. The current version is already in the right range of directness. The proposed version credits the Bible as the source of the teaching rather than just the vocabulary word.

---

### Change 4: "Sit down with your spouse tonight" -- expand the on-ramp

**Why:** The CIS says content should offer hope to "people who are behind, not just people who are ahead." The call to action (line 83) assumes a cooperative spouse. For a reader whose spouse is resistant to financial conversations, this could feel like a barrier rather than a step. A brief acknowledgment would strengthen the accessibility.

> **Current (lines 83-85):**
> Sit down with your spouse tonight -- or this weekend, when there's no rush. Show them this article if that's easier than explaining it cold. Then try the exercise from [Setting Your Hierarchy](/foundations/setting-your-hierarchy) together.

> **Proposed:**
> Sit down with your spouse tonight -- or this weekend, when there's no rush. If the conversation feels hard to start, show them this article. Sometimes it's easier to point at something than to explain it cold.
>
> If your spouse isn't ready for that conversation yet, start alone. Set up your own hierarchy. Check your own Safe-to-Spend. When the number starts bringing you peace instead of stress, the conversation gets easier -- because you're sharing something that works, not asking someone to try something theoretical.
>
> Then try the exercise from [Setting Your Hierarchy](/foundations/setting-your-hierarchy) together when you're both ready.
