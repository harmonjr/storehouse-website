## Review Summary

This is one of the strongest pieces in the Foundations series -- the opening scenario is vivid and immediately recognizable, the "circuit breaker" reframe is the kind of insight that changes how a reader thinks about raises, and the voice is warm and conversational throughout. At approximately 1,350 words (body text), it sits comfortably within the 800-1,500 range. The five proposed changes address a cross-reference that names an article awkwardly, a pillar mismatch in the frontmatter, a section heading that could land harder, one moment where the prose edges toward lecturing, and a structural note about the "One Step" section. None are blocking; all are refinements to an article that is already close to its best form.

## Voice Alignment

### Hits (strong voice moments)

- **Lines 13-20 (opening scenario):** "You get a raise. A good one -- $500 more a month, after taxes." The opening is specific, immediate, and earns the reader's attention with a scenario they have lived. The progression from "first month" to "second month" to "six months later" is paced perfectly. By line 20 ("It went everywhere. And nowhere specific."), the reader is nodding.

- **Line 25:** "Lifestyle creep doesn't arrive as a decision. It arrives as a series of upgrades so small that none of them registers as a choice." -- This is a truth candidate. The inversion of "decision" and "choice" against the reality of what happens is sharp and memorable.

- **Lines 27-28:** "A better coffee. A slightly nicer cut of meat at the grocery store. The premium tier instead of the basic." -- The catalog of small upgrades is exactly the kind of kitchen-table specificity that defines Storehouse voice. Each example is relatable without being condescending.

- **Line 31:** "You just let the water level rise one millimeter at a time until the margin was gone." -- Strong metaphor that captures the gradual, invisible nature of the problem. The "water level" image is fresh and not overused in personal finance writing.

- **Lines 37-38:** "The first time you order delivery, it's a convenience. The tenth time, it's a habit. The fiftieth time, cooking feels like a downgrade." -- Excellent progression. Three beats, but each one shifts the emotional register. The reader feels the ratchet effect in the sentence structure itself.

- **Line 73:** "Here's the reframe: a raise is a margin opportunity disguised as a spending opportunity." -- This is the article's central insight and it lands cleanly. Quotable independently.

- **Line 79:** "The people who build margin over time aren't usually the ones who make the most money. They're the ones who captured their raises before lifestyle creep could." -- Another truth candidate. Challenges the cultural assumption that wealth equals high income.

- **Line 101:** "You earned the raise. Now choose what it becomes." -- Clean, two-beat closing. Affirms the reader's work and turns the final sentence into an invitation, not a lecture.

### Misses (voice deviations)

- **Line 39:** "This is what psychologists call hedonic adaptation" -- The term "hedonic adaptation" is accurate but introduces academic jargon in a piece that otherwise stays conversational. The sentence immediately defines the concept, which helps, but the clinical term itself creates a brief shift in register. The Storehouse voice guidelines say "kitchen-table conversation about money, not a financial seminar." This moment briefly feels like a seminar. See Change 3.

- **Line 51:** "The subscription audit from 'The Subscription Audit' catches the charges you forgot about." -- Naming the article title and the concept with the same phrase creates an awkward construction. Other articles in the series handle cross-references more smoothly (e.g., "the hierarchy we wrote about in 'Give First'" in the budgeting apps article). See Change 2.

- **Lines 82-83:** "Lifestyle creep isn't a problem you solve once. It's the ongoing challenge for anyone whose income grows over time -- which is, hopefully, everyone." -- The parenthetical "which is, hopefully, everyone" is a light aside, but "the ongoing challenge" as a section heading feels generic and misses the opportunity to echo the article's stronger language. See Change 4.

## Proposed Changes

### Change 1: Update frontmatter pillar from "clarity" to "margin"

**Priority:** Medium
**Why:** Filter Question #2 / Cross-content consistency -- The article's dominant pillar is Margin. The core argument is about protecting financial breathing room from the silent force that erodes it. While Clarity is served (making the invisible mechanism visible), the article's structure, reframe ("a raise is a margin opportunity"), and closing all orient toward Margin. Five of the eight cross-reference articles correctly serve their stated pillar. This one would be more accurately tagged.

> **Current (line 7):**
> pillar: "clarity"

> **Proposed:**
> pillar: "margin"

The Content Integrity System's Margin pillar description (line 48) directly names lifestyle creep: "Addresses the forces that erode margin (lifestyle creep, subscription accumulation, keeping up)." This article is the full treatment of that force. It belongs under Margin.

---

### Change 2: Smooth the cross-reference to "The Subscription Audit"

**Priority:** Medium
**Why:** Voice alignment -- "The subscription audit from 'The Subscription Audit'" creates a redundant, awkward construction. Other articles in the series handle cross-references more cleanly (e.g., Article #3 references "the hierarchy we wrote about in 'Give First'"; Article #5 references "'The Subscription Audit'" by concept without repeating the phrase). The current phrasing reads like a reader stumbling over itself.

> **Current (line 51):**
> The subscription audit from "The Subscription Audit" catches the charges you forgot about.

> **Proposed:**
> The subscription audit we walk through in another article catches the charges you forgot about.

Alternative (more concise):

> **Proposed (alternative):**
> A subscription audit catches the charges you forgot about.

The first option preserves the hint that a companion article exists without the awkward self-reference. The alternative is more streamlined but loses the cross-reference entirely. Either is an improvement.

---

### Change 3: Remove the academic term "hedonic adaptation"

**Priority:** Medium
**Why:** Voice alignment -- The Content Integrity System says Storehouse voice is "a kitchen-table conversation about money, not a financial seminar" (line 92). "Hedonic adaptation" is a psychology textbook term. The concept is well-explained in the sentences that follow, but the clinical label is unnecessary and briefly shifts the register. The article can convey the same insight without the jargon.

> **Current (lines 39-40):**
> This is what psychologists call hedonic adaptation -- the tendency to return to a baseline level of satisfaction regardless of positive changes. The raise made you happy for a week.

> **Proposed:**
> That's the trap of the upgrade treadmill: you adjust to the new normal, and the satisfaction fades. The raise made you happy for a week.

This preserves the insight (satisfaction returns to baseline, creating a treadmill) while staying in the conversational register the rest of the article maintains. "Upgrade treadmill" picks up the "treadmill" metaphor the article uses one sentence later (line 40: "The treadmill keeps moving"), creating a cleaner throughline.

---

### Change 4: Strengthen the section heading "The Ongoing Challenge"

**Priority:** Medium
**Why:** Voice alignment -- "The Ongoing Challenge" is the most generic heading in the article. Every other heading carries specificity: "The Mechanism," "The New Normal," "A Raise Is a Margin Opportunity." This section deserves a heading that earns equal weight. The section is about the fact that lifestyle creep recurs with every raise, which is a recurring-temptation concept.

> **Current (line 82):**
> ## The Ongoing Challenge

> **Proposed:**
> ## Every Raise Asks the Same Question

This heading previews the section's central claim (line 84: "Every raise, every bonus, every new income stream presents the same question") and creates curiosity about what the question is. It also echoes the article's theme of raises as decision points.

---

### Change 5: Tighten the "One Step" opening to reduce throat-clearing

**Priority:** Medium
**Why:** Voice alignment / Prose rhythm -- Lines 93-94 open the closing section with two sentences of setup before arriving at the question. Compared to the "One Step" sections in other Foundations articles (which tend to open with an immediate action), this one pauses to orient the reader to a timeframe that is not essential. Tightening the opening gets to the reflective question faster.

> **Current (lines 93-94):**
> Think about your last raise or income increase. It might have been recent, or it might have been a year ago. Ask yourself: where did that money go?

> **Proposed:**
> Think about your last raise or income increase. Where did that money go?

The second sentence ("It might have been recent, or it might have been a year ago") adds no information the reader does not already know. Removing it sharpens the transition from prompt to question and matches the directness of other "One Step" sections in the series.
