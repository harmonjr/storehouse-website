## Review Summary

This is a strong, well-voiced article that does most things right -- warm opening, genuine reframes, practical scenario, earned conclusion. The single most important issue is the complete absence of biblical attribution. After the site-wide update to replace vague "faith" language with direct biblical sourcing, this article is the only Tier 1 foundation piece that never once mentions the Bible, God, or Scripture. The hierarchy is presented as a practical framework (which is fine) but its biblical origin is invisible (which contradicts the Integrity System's "Attributive, not vague" standard and the Craft Guide's Biblical Attribution Standard). One well-placed attribution would bring this into alignment without making the piece preachy.

## Proposed Changes

### Change 1: Add biblical attribution to the hierarchy reference

**Priority:** HIGH
**Why:** Biblical Attribution Standard (Craft Guide), "Attributive, not vague" voice guideline (Integrity System). This is the single most important change. After the site-wide update to replace vague "faith" language, this Tier 1 article is the only foundation piece that presents the hierarchy with zero biblical sourcing. The "Give First" companion article carries attribution (Genesis 41, 1 Peter 4:10), but this article will often be read independently. One moment of attribution -- woven naturally, not as a proof-text -- would bring it into compliance without changing the tone.

> **Current (lines 29-31):**
> Notice the order. It follows the hierarchy from "Give First, Save Second, Spend What Remains" -- giving comes out first, saving comes second, bills come third. What remains is yours. That's Safe-to-Spend.
>
> It's not what's left over. It's what remains after you've taken care of what matters most.

> **Proposed:**
> Notice the order. It follows the hierarchy from "Give First, Save Second, Spend What Remains" -- giving comes out first, saving comes second, bills come third. What remains is yours. That's Safe-to-Spend.
>
> The idea isn't new. The Bible calls it firstfruits -- honor God with the first of what you earn, not the leftovers (Proverbs 3:9). We just turned it into a formula.
>
> It's not what's left over. It's what remains after you've taken care of what matters most.

This adds one sentence and a parenthetical citation. It credits the source without preaching, uses the natural "firstfruits" language that the Craft Guide recommends, and transitions cleanly into the existing reframe line. It also establishes that the hierarchy has a deeper foundation than personal preference, which strengthens the article's authority.

### Change 2: Tighten the "Discipline Shift" section opening

**Priority:** Medium
**Why:** AI Tells (hedge phrase) and Craft Guide condescension radar. "Here's the real insight:" is a mild "let me explain" marker that positions the writer as revealing something to the reader rather than walking alongside them.

> **Current (line 81):**
> Here's the real insight: traditional budgets fail because they distribute discipline across every single purchase. Every trip to the store is a decision. Every category is a potential failure. The cognitive load is enormous, and eventually, everyone gives up.

> **Proposed:**
> Traditional budgets fail because they distribute discipline across every single purchase. Every trip to the store is a decision. Every category is a potential failure. The cognitive load is enormous, and eventually, everyone gives up.

Deleting four words. The insight is self-evident from the content that follows. It does not need an announcement.

**Priority:** Medium
**Why:** AI Tells (symmetrical structure). The same parenthetical appears on consecutive lines 94 and 95 with identical phrasing. Varying one breaks the pattern without losing the permission-giving function.

> **Current (lines 94-95):**

This variation echoes "Setting Your Hierarchy" (line 111: "Start where you are. Not where you think you should be"), creating a subtle cross-article connection while eliminating the mechanical repetition.

**AI Tells Count: 2 actionable, 2 minor (noted only)**

### Prose Rhythm: Strong

The piece varies sentence length well. Short punches ("That's a design problem." / "Wait until it is." / "No guilt. No guessing. No mental math at the register.") land after longer explanatory passages. The shoe-store scenario (lines 49-53) provides breathing room between conceptual sections. The three-word fragment "No guilt. No guessing. No mental math at the register." on line 45 is well-placed after the longer conditional logic of lines 37-44.

The one flat spot is the "Discipline Shift" section (lines 79-87), which runs at a fairly uniform medium sentence length without the short-long variation that makes other sections sing. The proposed deletion of "Here's the real insight:" would help by letting the section open with a longer declarative sentence rather than a short announcement.

### Reframes Found

- **"That's not a discipline problem. That's a design problem." (line 15)** -- Strong. Takes the reader's self-blame and redirects it to the system. This is the article's best line.

- **"The fight was never about the $40 dinner. It was about not having a shared definition of 'affordable.'" (line 65)** -- Strong. Reframes money fights from spending disagreements to measurement disagreements.

- **"An honest number you can trust is worth more than a comfortable number that lies to you." (line 75)** -- Strong. Inverts the assumption that a bigger number is better. The reader expected comfort; the article offers honesty as the superior value.

- **"Safe-to-Spend requires discipline in one decision: setting your priorities at the start." (line 83)** -- Moderate. Reframes budgeting from distributed discipline to concentrated discipline. Works structurally but is more explanatory than surprising.

**Reframe count: 3 strong, 1 moderate. Well above the "at least one" minimum.**

### Engagement: Pulls Forward

The opening earns the second paragraph -- the "You've tried... You've made... You've categorized..." build recognition, and the pivot to "That's not a discipline problem" creates a genuine reframe that makes you want to keep reading.

Each section creates curiosity for the next. "Why One Number Changes Everything" delivers the simplicity promise. "What It Actually Looks Like" grounds it in a specific scenario. "The Couples Equation" introduces a new stakeholder. "Why It Feels Wrong at First" earns trust by going to the uncomfortable place. "The Discipline Shift" provides the conceptual payoff. The actionable step closes without a CTA.

The one section that slightly loses momentum is "The Couples Equation" (lines 57-66). It introduces the couples angle well but resolves it too quickly -- three paragraphs, then done. The fuller treatment in "The Money Conversation" article is the right call, but this section could benefit from one more specific detail (a scenario, a moment) before resolving. That said, this is a minor observation, not a proposed change -- the section works, it just works less hard than the others.

### Condescension Check: Clean

No "simply put," "let me explain," "let's break this down." The one flag ("Here's the real insight:") is addressed in Change 2. The piece consistently treats the reader as an intelligent adult navigating a system that wasn't designed for them. Lines 15 ("That's a design problem"), 69-70 ("you're seeing the truth for the first time"), and 81 ("The cognitive load is enormous") all locate the problem in the system, not the reader.

### Guilt Radar: Clean

Read as someone $20,000 in debt, living paycheck to paycheck: the piece invites rather than shames. "Even if it's $0 today -- be honest" (line 94) is permission-giving. "It might surprise you. It might encourage you. Either way, it's telling you the truth" (line 99) acknowledges that the number could be hard to see. "The goal isn't to control your money" (line 101) removes the performance pressure. No guilt language found.

### Biblical Attribution: Needs Work

This is the primary craft issue. See Change 1 for full analysis. Summary: the article presents the hierarchy (a biblical principle) as a purely practical framework without ever naming its source. The Integrity System requires "Attributive, not vague," and the Craft Guide says to cite "when the principle originates from a specific passage." The firstfruits concept (Proverbs 3:9) is the direct biblical basis for "give first," and it is absent here.

The companion "Give First" article does carry strong attribution (Genesis 41, 1 Peter 4:10). But this article is Tier 1, will be read independently, and is the piece that introduces Safe-to-Spend -- the product's signature concept. Readers encountering Safe-to-Spend for the first time should understand where the ordering principle comes from. One sentence accomplishes this.

### Competitor Respect: Clean

No competitors named. "Budgeting apps" and "spreadsheets" are generic. "47 budget categories" critiques the approach, not a product. Line 17's reference to an "autopsy of last month's spending" critiques the backward-looking design philosophy, not a specific tool. Fully compliant with the Competitor Respect Standard.
