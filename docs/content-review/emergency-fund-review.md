## Proposed Changes

### Change 1: Broaden the Joseph Principle to match updated CIS

**Why:** CIS Section "The Joseph Principle" -- The updated CIS says the Joseph Principle is "broader than any single financial scenario" and means "building margin for life's uncertainty -- not just income fluctuations." The article's Joseph section (lines 71-78) tells the Genesis story well and credits God properly, but frames the emergency fund as "the same pattern at a smaller scale" of grain storage. This is accurate but narrows the principle to one application. The CIS says to keep the scope broad: "margin for whatever life brings, not a niche feature."

> **Current (lines 71-78):**
> ## The Joseph Pattern
>
> There's a story in Genesis about a man named Joseph who told Pharaoh to store grain during seven years of abundance so Egypt could survive seven years of famine. God gave Joseph the insight to see what was coming and the wisdom to prepare for it. The abundance wasn't for consuming. It was for storing.
>
> An emergency fund is the same pattern at a smaller scale. You're not storing grain -- you're storing margin. The good months, the months where nothing breaks and no one gets sick, are your abundance. Setting money aside during those months isn't pessimism. It's the same faithful preparation that kept a nation alive.
>
> Joseph didn't know exactly when the famine would come or how bad it would be. He just knew it was coming, because God told him so. You don't know when the car will break down or the furnace will quit. You just know it will. Building the fund is acting on what you know.

> **Proposed (lines 71-78):**
> ## The Joseph Pattern
>
> In Genesis 41, God revealed to Joseph that abundance would be followed by famine. Joseph's response -- store during plenty so you can survive the lean -- was not his own cleverness. The wisdom came from God. Joseph was the faithful executor.
>
> The emergency fund is this pattern woven into daily life. You're not storing grain -- you're storing margin. Not for one specific disaster, but for whatever life brings: the car repair, the medical bill, the job that ends without warning, the season that simply costs more than expected. The good months are your plenty. Setting money aside during those months is faithful preparation, not pessimism.
>
> Think in seasons, not just months. You don't know what the next hard season looks like or when it arrives. You just know it's coming. Building the fund is acting on what you know -- the same posture that kept a nation alive.

Changes: (1) Uses "seasons" language per CIS. (2) Broadens the examples beyond car/furnace to include job loss, medical bills, expensive seasons. (3) Credits God more explicitly as source of wisdom. (4) Incorporates the signature phrase "Think in seasons, not just months."

### Change 3: Minor craft tightening in "What Counts as an Emergency" section

**Why:** Craft Quality -- The "three qualifiers" (line 63) paragraph is solid, but the three examples that DON'T qualify (lines 65-66) use a symmetrical "X does not qualify" pattern that reads slightly AI-like (rule of three with matching structure). The section works well overall, so this is a low-priority suggestion.

> **Current (lines 65-66):**
> A good deal on a TV does not qualify. A weekend trip you didn't plan for does not qualify. A sale at your favorite store is not an emergency, no matter how good the price.

> **Proposed:**
> A good deal on a TV does not qualify. Neither does the weekend trip your friends just invited you on, no matter how badly you want to say yes. A sale -- any sale -- is never an emergency.

This varies the structure and adds a human moment ("how badly you want to say yes") instead of three parallel negations.
