## Review Summary

Strong craft with genuine voice and effective storytelling -- this is one of the better-written pieces in the foundation set. However, it has several alignment issues with the updated CIS: it names competitors directly (YNAB, Mint, Monarch) which risks violating the Competitor Respect Standard, it never mentions the Joseph Principle or biblical foundation with the directness now required, and it frames categories in a way that could imply Storehouse has no category tracking at all (it does -- for awareness). The piece also misses an opportunity to use the updated feature accuracy language.

## Proposed Changes

### Change 1: Competitor naming -- YNAB, Mint, Monarch named directly

**Why:** Competitor Respect Standard (CIS) and Craft Guide both say: critique the approach, not the product. The CIS says "Comparisons to other apps by name that feel like attacks" is a red line. This article names three competitors by name across multiple paragraphs. While the tone is respectful, the CIS substitution table recommends describing the approach rather than naming the product. The current framing is respectful enough that it's not a red line violation, but it's closer to the line than it needs to be.

> **Current (line 25):**
> YNAB teaches you to give every dollar a job. That's a powerful concept. But when you fall behind -- and everyone falls behind -- the recovery work starts to pile up.

> **Proposed:**
> Zero-based budgeting teaches you to give every dollar a job. That's a powerful concept. But when you fall behind -- and everyone falls behind -- the recovery work starts to pile up. You have to shuffle money between categories, pull from one to cover another, and the whole structure unravels into a game of whack-a-mole. The approach didn't fail. Your energy to maintain 47 categories did.

> **Current (line 27):**
> Mint was brilliant at showing you where money went. But it was always backward-looking...

> **Proposed:**
> Passive tracking tools are brilliant at showing you where money went. But they're always backward-looking -- a detailed autopsy of last month's spending. Knowing you spent $387 on dining out doesn't help you at 6pm on a Tuesday when you're wondering if tonight is okay. The data is comprehensive. The peace is absent.

> **Current (line 29):**
> Monarch is beautiful and investment-forward -- great for people who've already built margin and want to grow wealth.

> **Proposed:**
> Wealth-tracking apps are beautiful and investment-forward -- great for people who've already built margin and want to grow wealth. But if you're still trying to figure out whether you can afford dinner out this week, a net worth tracker isn't the tool you need yet.

### Change 2: Missing Joseph Principle reference

**Why:** The updated CIS identifies the Joseph Principle as "a key differentiator alongside Safe-to-Spend and the hierarchy." This article discusses stewardship vs. control at length but never names the Joseph Principle or connects Storehouse's margin-building to the biblical pattern. The "Stewardship, Not Control" section (line 69) is the natural place.

> **Current (lines 71-77):**
> Here's the deeper difference: most budgeting apps assume the goal is to control your money. Storehouse assumes the goal is to steward it.
>
> Control is about grip -- monitoring, restricting, optimizing. It works when you have the energy, and it collapses when you don't. That's why most people quit their budgets by February.
>
> Biblical stewardship is about posture. It's about deciding what you value, putting those things first, and living freely within what remains -- trusting that God provides the rest. It doesn't require you to track every coffee. It requires you to make a few important decisions and trust what flows from them.

> **Proposed:**
> Here's the deeper difference: most budgeting apps assume the goal is to control your money. Storehouse assumes the goal is to steward it.
>
> Control is about grip -- monitoring, restricting, optimizing. It works when you have the energy, and it collapses when you don't. That's why most people quit their budgets by February.
>
> Biblical stewardship is about posture. It's about deciding what you value, putting those things first, and living freely within what remains. In Genesis 41, God gave Joseph wisdom to store during plenty so Egypt could survive the lean years. That's the pattern Storehouse is built on -- not anxious control, but faithful preparation. Think in seasons, not just months. Build margin for whatever life brings. It doesn't require you to track every coffee. It requires you to make a few important decisions and trust what flows from them.

### Change 3: Biblical directness -- faith attribution is present but could be stronger

**Why:** Voice Guidelines (CIS) now say "Faith is stated directly" and "Be attributive, not vague." Line 47 says "It's a biblical principle" which is good but could be slightly more direct per the updated standard. Line 75 says "trusting that God provides the rest" which is appropriately direct. The issue is minor -- the piece is reasonably direct, but the updated CIS wants "The Bible teaches..." style attribution where the principle is named.

> **Current (line 47):**
> That's not a budgeting trick. It's a biblical principle -- steward what you've been given, starting with what matters most.

> **Proposed:**
> That's not a budgeting trick. The Bible teaches that we steward what we've been given, starting with what matters most. Storehouse turns that conviction into a formula.

### Change 4: "47 categories" used in both this article and "The One Number" article

**Why:** Cross-content consistency. This article uses "47 categories" on lines 25, 37, 57, and 65. "The One Number That Tells You the Truth" also uses "47 categories" on lines 17 and 37. The repeated specific number across articles risks feeling like a scripted talking point rather than authentic voice. Consider varying the number in one article or making it less specific in the piece where it carries less narrative weight.

> **Current (line 57):**
> Instead of 47 categories, Storehouse shows you one number

> **Proposed:**
> Instead of dozens of categories, Storehouse shows you one number

Note: Keep "47 categories" in the opening paragraph (line 13) where the reader is listing out their own categories -- that's where the specific number feels lived-in. Replace other instances with natural variation.

### Change 5: Categories framing needs precision

**Why:** Feature Accuracy (CIS) says Storehouse "auto-categorizes for awareness" but has "no category budgets." The article's framing of "one number instead of forty-seven" could imply Storehouse ignores categories entirely. The CIS signature phrase is: "Categories are the rearview mirror, not the steering wheel."

> **Current (lines 57-67):**
> This is where Safe-to-Spend comes in. Instead of 47 categories, Storehouse shows you one number: what's left after giving, saving, and bills have been handled.
>
> You don't need to know if dinner comes from the "dining" budget or the "entertainment" budget. You don't need to check whether you've hit your coffee limit. You need to know one thing: is my number above zero?
>
> If yes, spend freely. The priorities are already handled.
>
> If no, wait.
>
> The simplicity isn't laziness. It's the result of concentrating discipline where it matters -- in the hierarchy, not in the categories. You make three decisions at the start (how much to give, save, and set aside for bills), and every purchase after that is one question.

> **Proposed:**
> This is where Safe-to-Spend comes in. Instead of managing dozens of category budgets, Storehouse shows you one number: what's left after giving, saving, and bills have been handled.
>
> You don't need to check whether dinner comes from the "dining" budget or the "entertainment" budget. You don't need to monitor your coffee limit. You need to know one thing: is my number above zero?
>
> If yes, spend freely. The priorities are already handled.
>
> If no, wait.
>
> Storehouse still tracks categories -- you can see where your money went after the fact. But categories are the rearview mirror, not the steering wheel. One number tells you what you can spend. The categories show you the patterns later, when you're curious, not when you're standing at the register. The simplicity isn't laziness. It's the result of concentrating discipline where it matters -- in the hierarchy, not in category budgets.
