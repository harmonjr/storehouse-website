**Reviewed:** 2026-02-14
**Reviewer:** content-reviewer agent
**Verdict:** Publish-ready with revisions

## Review Summary

A strong Tier 2 Clarity article with an effective opening, a genuinely useful three-step process, and several quotable reframes. The piece earns its conclusions and avoids AI tells almost entirely. The primary integrity issue is a pattern of vague stewardship language that floats free of its biblical foundation -- the word "stewardship" appears five times but the Bible is never named as its source, which contradicts the Integrity System's "Attributive, not vague" guideline. The craft is solid: prose rhythm varies well, the "treasure hunt, not a diet" reframe works, and the table provides visual breathing room. Six proposed changes -- one high-priority (biblical attribution), five medium (redundancy, math clarity, repeated truth, cross-reference format, a near-prescriptive line).

## Voice Alignment

### Hits

- **Lines 13-17:** "We found $73 a month in subscriptions we'd forgotten about. Not subscriptions we were using and decided to cancel. Subscriptions we didn't know we were paying for." Three specific, relatable examples follow. Kitchen-table storytelling at its best.

- **Lines 25-26:** "You signed up once. You stopped using the service. The charge continues. That's not a bill. That's a leak." Short, punchy, rhythmic. A genuine reframe delivered in five sentences. Pull-quote worthy.

- **Line 27:** "Reclaiming forgotten subscriptions feels like finding a $20 bill in your coat pocket -- except it's $50 or $100, and it shows up every single month." Perfect analogy. Warm, specific, shifts the emotional register from cutting to finding.

- **Lines 53-55:** "Not 'do I use it sometimes?' Not 'might I need it eventually?' Would you, right now, hand over your credit card and sign up fresh?" Excellent practical writing -- the two disqualifying questions sharpen the real one.

- **Lines 80-81:** "No single charge feels worth the effort to cancel. Together, they're a car payment." The core insight of subscription creep in two sentences.

- **Lines 88-96 ("Where the Found Money Goes"):** "The hierarchy offers a different path" (line 88) and "Any of those options is stewardship" (line 96) use stewardship language without ever grounding it. The word "stewardship" does real work in this article (lines 96, 98, 104, 120) but the reader never learns that this is a biblical concept. This article uses the language five times without attribution once. A Tier 2 article can assume some reader familiarity with the hierarchy from Tier 1, but the word "stewardship" itself needs grounding somewhere in the piece. See Change 1.

- **Line 100:** "You Can't Steward What You Can't See" -- section heading uses the founding truth. Line 100 body restates it. Line 122 closes with it. Three uses of the same founding truth in one article. The heading and closing are both strong; the body restatement is the weakest of the three. See Change 4.

## Proposed Changes

### Change 1: Ground "stewardship" in its biblical source

**Priority:** High
**Why:** Voice alignment -- The Integrity System's "Attributive, not vague" guideline says: "When a principle comes from the Bible, say so. 'The Bible teaches...' and 'God's wisdom' are better than 'ancient wisdom' or 'faith traditions.' Give credit where it's due." The word "stewardship" appears five times in this article (lines 88, 96, 98, 104, 120) but is never connected to its biblical origin. By Tier 2, the reader may have encountered Article 1's grounding of stewardship (which cites 1 Peter 4:10 and Genesis 41), but this article should not float entirely free of attribution. The "Where the Found Money Goes" section (lines 82-96) is the natural place to anchor it -- it is where the practical audit connects to the larger Storehouse philosophy. One sentence is enough. The Craft Guide's Biblical Attribution Standard says one or two citations per article is the sweet spot (line 291), and this article currently has zero.

> **Current (lines 88-89):**
> The hierarchy offers a different path.
>
> That $78 could increase your giving.

> **Proposed (lines 88-89):**
> The hierarchy offers a different path. Stewardship -- the biblical idea that what we have isn't ours to consume but ours to manage faithfully -- means found money is an opportunity, not a windfall.
>
> That $78 could increase your giving.

This grounds the concept once, naturally, without turning the section into a sermon. The rest of the article's uses of "stewardship" can then stand on their own because the reader knows the source. The phrasing echoes the Integrity System's core definition (line 9) and the Craft Guide's posture of Scripture as invitation (line 308).

### Change 6: Soften the near-prescriptive "cancel it today" line

**Priority:** Medium
**Why:** Voice alignment / Guilt radar -- Line 57 says "If no, cancel it. Today. Not next week. Not after you 'check one more time.'" This is the most prescriptive moment in the article. It works in context because the surrounding prose (lines 59-61) immediately softens it with "every subscription you cancel can be restarted." But the tone of "Today. Not next week." sits slightly outside the Storehouse voice of "encouraging, not punishing." The Craft Guide's guilt radar flags "If you just..." language that implies the problem is simple. This is adjacent -- it implies the solution is simple (just cancel now), when the reader may have legitimate reasons for hesitation (shared accounts, annual plans, cancellation friction).

> **Current (line 57):**
> If no, cancel it. Today. Not next week. Not after you "check one more time." The reason these charges persist is because canceling requires a decision and inertia doesn't. Make the decision now while you're looking at the number.

> **Proposed (line 57):**
> If no, cancel it. The reason these charges persist is because canceling requires a decision and inertia doesn't. Right now, while you're looking at the number, is the easiest moment to make that decision.

This preserves the urgency (act now while you're paying attention) but removes the slightly scolding "Today. Not next week." cadence. The reframe from "you must act now" to "now is the easiest moment" keeps the encouragement without the edge.

## Craft Quality

### AI Tells Found

- **None significant.** This piece is remarkably clean of AI patterns. No hedge phrases, no filler transitions, no rule-of-three obsession, no symmetrical structure, no passive hedging. The one mild flag: "Before we go any further, let's get the framing right" (line 23) is a soft throat-clearing transition, but it reads naturally because the reframe it introduces (treasure hunt, not diet) genuinely needs framing before the process steps begin. Not flagging for revision.

### Prose Rhythm: Strong

The piece varies sentence length effectively throughout. The opening paragraph stacks three specific examples in a longer sentence, then punches with "Seventy-three dollars a month" (line 17, spelled out -- smart choice that forces the reader to feel the number). The three-step process section is clear and scannable. The table provides visual breathing room between prose-heavy sections. Short fragments ("That's a leak." "That's freedom.") land well against longer analytical sentences.

### Reframes Found

- **"Treasure hunt, not a diet" (line 21):** Strong. Takes the reader's assumption (audit = deprivation) and inverts it. Well-placed at the top before the process begins.
- **"That's not a bill. That's a leak." (lines 25-26):** Strong. Reframes forgotten subscriptions from passive obligation to active problem. Quotable.
- **"That's not austerity. That's freedom." (line 122):** Strong closer. Echoes the Integrity System's signature phrase ("The hierarchy isn't punishment. It's freedom.") while making it specific to the audit context.

Three genuine reframes, well-spaced. The piece passes the "Changed Inside" test.

### Engagement: Pulls forward

Each section creates curiosity for the next. The opening problem leads to the reframe, the reframe leads to the process, the process leads to the math, the math leads to "where does this money go?" -- which connects to the hierarchy. The final section appropriately lowers the barrier from the full three-month audit to a thirty-day scan. The reader never has a natural exit point until the end.

### Condescension Check: Clear

No "simply put," "let me explain," or "let's break this down." The reader is treated as a capable adult who can follow a three-step process. The "Would I sign up for this today?" test (line 51) gives the reader a tool rather than a lecture. The only borderline moment is line 57 ("Today. Not next week.") which edges toward telling the reader what to do -- addressed in Change 6.

### Show vs. Tell Ratio: Good

- Opening story (lines 13-18): shows the problem through personal experience
- Table (lines 67-76): shows the math visually
- "Would I sign up for this today?" (line 51): gives a tool, not a sermon
- "Where the Found Money Goes" (lines 82-96): shows the hierarchy in action with specific dollar amounts
- Minor weakness: the closing section (lines 114-122) leans slightly toward telling ("Because the first step toward better stewardship of your money is always the same: see where it's actually going.") but the closing truth earns it.
