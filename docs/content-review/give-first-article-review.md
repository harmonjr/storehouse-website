## Proposed Changes

### Change 1: Strengthen the opening sentence

**Why:** Voice alignment (minor). The opening is good but "pay yourself first" is insider personal finance language. The manifesto should open with something every reader recognizes.

> **Current (line 13):**
> Most financial advice starts with "pay yourself first." Build your emergency fund. Max out your 401k. Then, if there's anything left, maybe consider giving something away.

> **Proposed:**
> Most financial advice starts in the same place: take care of yourself first. Build your emergency fund. Max out your 401k. Then, if there's anything left, maybe consider giving something away.

**Priority:** Medium. The current version works. The proposed version is slightly more universal.

---

### Change 2: Remove the soft disclaimer in paragraph two

**Why:** Craft quality -- hedge phrase. "There's nothing wrong with that advice" is throat-clearing that weakens the manifesto posture. The article is arguing for a different order; pre-emptively validating the old order dilutes the argument before it begins.

> **Current (lines 15-16):**
> There's nothing wrong with that advice. It's responsible. It's practical. And for millions of people, it produces a life that looks financially healthy from the outside.

> **Proposed:**
> That advice is responsible. It's practical. And for millions of people, it produces a life that looks financially healthy from the outside.

**Priority:** Medium. The current version is gracious, which fits Storehouse voice. But "there's nothing wrong with that advice" is the kind of hedge that tells the reader you are about to argue against something you don't fully believe you should argue against. Removing the first sentence keeps the grace (acknowledging it's responsible and practical) while sharpening the stance.

---

### Change 3: Cut the hedge before the math question

**Why:** Craft quality -- hedge phrase. "You might wonder" distances the writer from the question. Just ask it.

> **Current (line 64):**
> You might wonder: does the math actually change?

> **Proposed:**
> Does the math actually change?

**Priority:** Medium. Small edit, noticeable improvement. The question is strong on its own.

---

### Change 4: Tighten "The Surprising Result" section opening

**Why:** Craft quality -- the "Here's what we've found" construction is slightly generic. The insight that follows is strong enough to lead.

> **Current (lines 85-86):**
> Here's what we've found, both in our own lives and in talking to others who've tried this: people who prioritize generosity often end up with more margin, not less.

> **Proposed:**
> People who prioritize generosity often end up with more margin, not less. We've seen it in our own finances and heard it from others who've tried this order.

**Priority:** Medium. Leads with the surprising claim (which earns the section title) and then grounds it in experience. The original buries the insight behind a preamble.

---

### Change 5: Reconsider section heading "The Surprising Result"

**Why:** Voice alignment -- "The Surprising Result" reads slightly clinical/listicle. The section is about generosity producing freedom, which is the emotional heart of the piece.

> **Current (line 84):**
> ## The Surprising Result

> **Proposed:**
> ## What Generosity Actually Produces

**Priority:** Low. The current heading works. The proposed heading is more specific and more Storehouse-voiced. Either is acceptable.

### Prose Rhythm: Strong

The sentence length varies naturally. Short declarative punches ("We think it's because the order is wrong." / "He wasn't wealthy. He was a steward.") follow longer, more complex sentences. Paragraphs range from two sentences to five. The piece reads aloud with natural cadence.

Specific moments of strong rhythm:
- Line 29: "Nobody teaches this order. Nobody chooses it deliberately." -- The repetition of "nobody" creates momentum that the following long sentence disrupts productively.
- Lines 56-57: Two short sentences after a narrative paragraph. The brevity signals importance.
- Line 72: A long sentence with a colon pivot. The payoff ("freedom from money's control") lands precisely because the setup was patient.

One rhythm concern: the "Why the Order Matters" section (lines 62-72) runs five paragraphs of roughly similar length. The ideas are different, but the visual and rhythmic pattern is uniform. A shorter paragraph somewhere in this section would create breathing room.

### Reframes Found

- **"The order you assign your dollars reveals the order you assign your values."** (line 44) -- THE reframe. This is the founding truth specified in the Content Integrity System and the canon plan. It works because it starts from something familiar (you choose where money goes) and reveals something hidden (that choice is a values statement). Strong. Earned by the preceding setup.

- **"That shift is the difference between budgeting and stewardship."** (line 48) -- A secondary reframe that lands because "stewardship" has not yet been defined. The reader's curiosity is piqued, and the next section delivers.

- **"He wasn't wealthy. He was a steward."** (line 56) -- A miniature reframe of the Joseph story. Most people think of Joseph as powerful or successful. Five words recast him as a manager of what wasn't his. Concise, surprising, earned.

- **"The person who starts at 1% isn't failing at 10% -- they're choosing to begin."** (line 80) -- Reframes the "I'm not doing enough" feeling into agency. This is the grace moment the article needs.

**Reframe count: 4.** The canon plan required at least one. The primary reframe (line 44) is well-placed at the hinge of the article, exactly where the argument pivots from "here's the problem" to "here's what changes."

### Engagement: Pulls forward

- **Opening:** The first three paragraphs earn the fourth. The reader recognizes the "pay yourself first" advice, nods at its respectability, and then the turn ("why does it still feel so heavy?") creates genuine curiosity.
- **"The Default Order" section:** Quick, concrete, familiar. The reader sees themselves in the three-item list. The closing line ("the default produces anxiety") names what they have felt but not articulated.
- **"The Flip" section:** The hierarchy is presented cleanly. The core reframe (line 44) is the "changed inside" moment.
- **"What Stewardship Actually Means" section:** The Joseph story gives the article depth beyond a finance listicle. A non-financial reader would still find this section compelling.
- **"It's Not About Being Perfect":** This is where the article earns trust. Without this section, the hierarchy could feel like another rule to fail at. With it, the hierarchy becomes aspirational rather than punitive.
- **"One Step You Can Take Today":** Clean, actionable, non-threatening. "You don't need to change anything yet. Just notice the order." This is the right ask for a manifesto -- awareness before action.
- **Closing:** "That's not a punishment. It's the most peaceful way we've found to handle money." -- Ends with warmth and conviction. Not a CTA. Not a sales pitch. Encouragement.

The only section that slightly loses momentum is "Why the Order Matters" (lines 62-72). The argument is sound, but it covers familiar ground (spend first = less for giving/saving; give first = priorities handled). A reader who already agreed after "The Flip" might skim here. This is not a structural problem -- the section serves readers who need the mechanism explained -- but it is the lowest-energy section in an otherwise consistently engaging piece.
