### Change 1 (revised): Add breathing room in "Why the Order Matters" section

**Why:** Prose rhythm -- the section from lines 63-72 runs five paragraphs of similar length. The ideas are distinct, but the visual and rhythmic pattern is uniform. Breaking the "When you give first" paragraph (line 70) into two shorter beats would create variety.

> **Current (lines 70-71):**
> When you give first, the opposite happens. Generosity is the first act, not the last. Saving is the second decision, not the hopeful leftover. By the time you reach spending, the priorities are handled. What remains is genuinely yours.

> **Proposed:**
> When you give first, the opposite happens. Generosity is the first act, not the last. Saving is the second decision, not the hopeful leftover.
>
> By the time you reach spending, the priorities are handled. What remains is genuinely yours.

**Priority:** Medium. Creates a visual and rhythmic pause before the payoff line ("What remains is genuinely yours"). The paragraph break lets that final sentence land with more weight.

---

### Change 2: Soften "prosperity gospel magic"

**Why:** Competitor Respect / Voice alignment. "Prosperity gospel magic" is doing important guard-rail work (the integrity system flags prosperity gospel as a red line), but "magic" adds a dismissive edge that could make a reader who has heard prosperity theology feel mocked rather than understood. The sentence already does its job with "giving doesn't guarantee wealth" -- the guardrail holds without the loaded word.

> **Current (line 86):**
> Not because of some prosperity gospel magic -- giving doesn't guarantee wealth, and anyone who tells you otherwise is selling something.

> **Proposed:**
> Not because giving guarantees wealth -- it doesn't, and anyone who tells you otherwise is selling something.

**Priority:** Medium. The current version names prosperity gospel by name, which is useful for clarity but edges toward the "We Are Not" list's warning against being dismissive. The proposed version draws the same line ("it doesn't, and anyone who tells you otherwise is selling something") without naming the movement. Either works; the proposed version is slightly warmer toward readers who may have encountered prosperity theology in their churches without choosing it themselves.

---

### Change 3: Tighten the generosity section opening

**Why:** Craft quality -- the section heading "What Generosity Actually Produces" (line 84) is strong, but the opening paragraph (lines 85-86) buries the surprising claim behind attribution of the evidence. Lead with the claim.

> **Current (lines 85-86):**
> People who prioritize generosity often end up with more margin, not less. We've seen it in our own finances and heard it from others who've tried this order.

> **Proposed:**
> People who prioritize generosity end up with more margin, not less. We've seen it in our own finances and heard it from others who've tried this order.

**Priority:** Low. The word "often" is a hedge. The sentence is stronger without it. The second sentence ("We've seen it") already qualifies the claim with appropriate humility. That said, "often" is an honest qualifier -- not every family's experience matches -- so this is genuinely optional.

---

### Change 4: Strengthen the rule-of-three in line 90

**Why:** AI Tells -- rule-of-three watch. Lines 89-90 list three parallel outcomes of generosity: "you break the scarcity mindset," "you make intentional choices," "you build contentment as a practice." This is followed by a fourth item (line 90) that breaks the pattern, which is good. But the three items preceding it all begin with "You" and have nearly identical sentence structure. Varying the construction of one would reduce the AI-pattern feel.

> **Current (lines 89-90):**
> When you give first, you break the scarcity mindset that drives overspending. You make intentional choices about what remains. You build contentment as a practice, not a feeling you're chasing. And you stop measuring your financial health by how much you've accumulated and start measuring it by how well you've stewarded what you have.

> **Proposed:**
> When you give first, the scarcity mindset that drives overspending starts to loosen. You make intentional choices about what remains. Contentment becomes a practice, not a feeling you're chasing. And you stop measuring your financial health by how much you've accumulated and start measuring it by how well you've stewarded what you have.

**Priority:** Medium. The current version is not bad -- four items already breaks the rule-of-three pattern. But the three identical "You [verb]" constructions are the kind of symmetry the Craft Guide flags. Varying the first and third items breaks the pattern without losing the content.
