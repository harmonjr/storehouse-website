## Proposed Changes

### Change 1: Joseph Principle -- align with updated CIS language

**Why:** The Joseph Principle section (CIS) now says to use "seasons" language ("plenty seasons / lean seasons"), credit God as the source of the wisdom, and keep the scope broad ("margin for whatever life brings, not a niche feature for freelancers"). Line 83 references Genesis 41 and credits God -- that's good. But it doesn't use the "seasons" language or connect to the updated key phrase "Think in seasons, not just months." This article is the perfect place for that language because the reader IS in a lean season.

> **Current (lines 82-84):**
> Stewardship doesn't require abundance. In Genesis 41, God gave Joseph wisdom to steward during famine, not just during plenty. The practice is the same whether you're working with $4,000 a month or $40,000 -- decide what matters, put those things first, and live intentionally on what remains.

> **Proposed:**
> Stewardship doesn't require abundance. In Genesis 41, God gave Joseph wisdom to store during plenty so Egypt could survive the lean years ahead. The wisdom was God's. Joseph was the faithful executor. You might be in a lean season right now -- and the practice is the same whether you're working with $4,000 a month or $40,000. Decide what matters, put those things first, and live intentionally on what remains. Think in seasons, not just months. This season won't last forever.

### Change 2: "The hierarchy works at zero" -- add categories clarity

**Why:** Feature Accuracy (CIS) says Storehouse "auto-categorizes for awareness" with "no category budgets." This article doesn't mention categories at all, which is fine for the most part -- it's focused on the hierarchy at small numbers. But the Safe-to-Spend explanation on line 45 could benefit from the updated framing to avoid the impression that Storehouse is featureless at the low end.

> **Current (line 45):**
> And Safe-to-Spend -- the number that remains after giving, saving, and bills -- might be uncomfortably small. But it's true. And a true number, even a small one, is something you can work with.

> **Proposed:**
> And Safe-to-Spend -- the number that remains after giving, saving, and bills -- might be uncomfortably small. But it's true. And a true number, even a small one, is something you can work with. You don't need to manage dozens of category budgets. One honest number tells you where you stand.

Note: This is a light touch. The article's focus on simplicity and hope should not be diluted with feature explanations. One sentence is sufficient.

## Craft Quality

### AI Tells Found
- **Minor hedge phrase**: "Here is the most counterintuitive part:" (line 39) -- This is close to "It's important to note" territory but serves a genuine pivot. The sentence that follows IS counterintuitive, so the setup earns its place. Borderline -- could be cut ("The hierarchy works even when the numbers are small. Even when some of them are zero.") but the current version works.
- **Near rule-of-three**: Lines 80-81 list "A medical crisis. A job loss. A divorce. A late start. A season of mistakes. An economy that didn't cooperate." -- Six items. Good. The count varies naturally and avoids the AI-three pattern.
- **No significant AI tells detected.** The piece reads as written by someone who has been in this position.

### Prose Rhythm: Strong
The opening is specific and grounding -- "$14" is a detail that no AI would choose. The sentence "Stay. This one is for you." (line 17) is a perfect two-sentence paragraph that breaks the rhythm with intention. The "$25 a month" thread is woven through multiple sections without feeling repetitive -- each appearance adds something. The closing paragraph builds to "Especially from here." which lands because the entire piece has been building toward permission to begin.

### Reframes Found
- "Zero isn't a grade. It's a location." (line 33) -- The strongest reframe in the piece. The reader came in seeing zero as a verdict; this line reframes it as a starting point. Concise, true, memorable.
- "The person with $0 in savings who sets up a hierarchy today is closer to financial peace than the person with $10,000 in savings who spends without intention." (line 33) -- Challenges the assumption that more money = more peace. The inversion is genuine and surprising.
- "Starting at 1% isn't failing at 10%." (line 101) -- Repeated from "Give First" but earned here through the full treatment. In the original article it was a sentence; here it's a thesis.

### Engagement: Pulls forward
The opening drops the reader into a specific financial moment ($14 in savings, rent in five days). The second paragraph anticipates the reader's objection ("Give first, save second -- that sounds great for people who have something to give") and addresses it before they close the tab. "Stay. This one is for you." is the hook. Every section after that delivers on the promise. The piece never loses the reader it fought to keep.

### The "Changed Inside" Test: PASS
The reader enters believing their balance defines them. They leave with "Zero isn't a grade. It's a location." That's a genuine internal shift. The conclusion is earned through the journey -- the math ($25/month = $300/year = a car repair), the Joseph reference, the acknowledgment of how hard starting is. The piece shows rather than tells.
