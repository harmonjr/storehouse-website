## Review Summary

A well-crafted piece with strong emotional specificity and a compelling worked example. The opening paragraph (the bonus arriving on a Thursday) is one of the best openings in the Storehouse collection -- it drops the reader into a recognizable moment and names the emotional sequence precisely. The article applies the hierarchy to windfalls and irregular income effectively, and the closing call to action is practical. However, two significant issues need attention: (1) the article was moved from Foundations to Blog but still reads as a foundations-length deep dive at ~1,500 words, which is fine for the blog format; and (2) the Joseph Principle is implied but never named, even though this article is the most natural home for it. The CIS now has an explicit section on the Joseph Principle and says to use "seasons" language and credit God as the source of the wisdom. The article references stewardship (line 60) and the Bible (line 60) but does not connect to the Joseph Principle by name, which is a missed opportunity given the updated messaging direction.

## Proposed Changes

### Change 1: Name the Joseph Principle explicitly

**Priority:** High
**Why:** Joseph Principle section (CIS). The updated CIS has a dedicated section on the Joseph Principle and lists it as "a key differentiator alongside Safe-to-Spend and the hierarchy." This article is the most natural home for the Joseph Principle in the blog collection -- it deals with surplus, abundance, lean seasons, and the question of what to do with unexpected income. Yet it never mentions Joseph, Genesis 41, or the Joseph Principle by name. The freelancers article (a companion piece) does reference Joseph. The two should be consistent.

> **Current (lines 55-62):**
> Here's the reframe: a windfall isn't free money. It's a stewardship test with higher stakes and no monthly reset.
>
> Regular income gives you practice -- every month, you run the hierarchy, and if you miss a month, the next one comes around. Windfalls don't work that way. The bonus arrives once. The tax refund comes once a year. The freelance check clears and that project is over. There's no next month to try again with that specific money.
>
> That's not pressure. It's clarity. The Bible calls this stewardship -- faithfully managing what God has entrusted to you, whether the amount is expected or not.

> **Proposed:**
> Here's the reframe: a windfall isn't free money. It's a stewardship test with higher stakes and no monthly reset.
>
> In Genesis 41, God showed Joseph that abundance would be followed by scarcity. Joseph's response was to store with foresight during the good years so Egypt could survive the lean ones. That's the Joseph Principle -- and it doesn't just apply to seven-year economic cycles. It applies to the bonus that arrives once. The tax refund that comes once a year. The freelance check that clears and then the project is over. There's no next season to try again with that specific money.
>
> That's not pressure. It's clarity. The Bible calls this stewardship -- faithfully managing what God has entrusted to you, whether the amount is expected or not. Think in seasons, not just months. The windfall is your plenty season. What you do with it builds the margin for whatever comes next.

This adds the Joseph Principle by name, credits God as the source, uses "seasons" language, and connects the broader principle to the specific article topic. It also incorporates the CIS signature phrase "Think in seasons, not just months."

The three-beat sequence -- "excitement, responsibility, guilt" (line 11) -- works because it names an emotional progression the reader has lived. The opening drops the reader into a specific, recognizable moment (the bonus arriving on a Thursday). The emotional sequence (excitement, responsibility, guilt) creates identification. "That sequence... is what happens when money arrives without a plan" (line 12) names the problem. The article then alternates between diagnosis (why windfalls vanish), prescription (apply the hierarchy), worked example (the table), emotional permission (enjoy some of it), and practical action (the closing step). The reader never sits in one mode long enough to disengage.
