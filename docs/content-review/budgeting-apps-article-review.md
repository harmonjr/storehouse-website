## Review Summary

This article has been significantly revised since the February 12 review. The most notable change: it now names competitors by name (YNAB, Mint, Monarch) where the previous version used only generic references. The naming is handled with genuine respect and critiques the approach rather than the product or people, which clears the Competitor Respect Standard -- but two moments warrant attention. The biblical attribution is adequate: "biblical stewardship" and "God" are invoked directly on lines 71-75, avoiding the vague "faith language" pattern the site recently corrected. However, the article leans secular for most of its length and only arrives at explicit biblical grounding in the final third, which means a reader who stops early would encounter the hierarchy as merely a preference rather than a conviction rooted in Scripture. Five proposed changes, none structural. The single most important issue is a sentence on line 25 that attributes emotional judgment ("punishes you") to YNAB's system in a way that edges from approach critique toward product critique.

## Proposed Changes

### Change 1: Soften "the system punishes you" (YNAB reference)

**Priority:** High
**Why:** Competitor Respect Standard. The word "punishes" attributes emotional intent to YNAB's design. The Craft Guide's substitution table says to write "Zero-based budgeting can feel like a disappointed parent" rather than "YNAB makes you feel guilty." The current phrasing is closer to the "don't write" column than the "write instead" column.

> **Current (line 25):**
> YNAB teaches you to give every dollar a job. That's a powerful concept. But when you fall behind -- and everyone falls behind -- the system punishes you.

> **Proposed:**
> YNAB teaches you to give every dollar a job. That's a powerful concept. But when you fall behind -- and everyone falls behind -- the recovery work starts to pile up.

**Rationale:** "The recovery work starts to pile up" describes the user's experience without attributing malice to the system. The next sentence about "roll with the punches" and whack-a-mole still carries the emotional weight. The word "punishes" is doing unnecessary damage to an otherwise respectful paragraph.

---

### Change 2: Vary the "47 categories" count

**Priority:** Medium
**Why:** Cross-content consistency. The number 47 appears in both this article and Article #2. Article #2 should own the specific number since it uses it in the title-adjacent metadata.

> **Current (line 57):**
> Instead of 47 categories, Storehouse shows you one number: what's left after giving, saving, and bills have been handled.

> **Proposed:**
> Instead of tracking every category, Storehouse shows you one number: what's left after giving, saving, and bills have been handled.

**Rationale:** The "one number vs. many categories" contrast is preserved without repeating the exact count. The opening paragraph (line 13) already establishes the category-overload feeling through the list itself ("groceries, dining out, gas, entertainment, subscriptions, household supplies, personal care, gifts, clothing, coffee"). The reader does not need the number 47 repeated to feel the contrast.

---

### Change 3: Tighten the Mint reference for accuracy

**Priority:** Low
**Why:** Factual accuracy and competitor respect. Mint was shut down in January 2024 and its users were migrated to Credit Karma. Describing Mint in present tense ("was brilliant at showing you") is fine -- the past tense is already correct. But line 28 says "The data was comprehensive. The peace was absent." The second sentence attributes a negative outcome to the product rather than the approach.

> **Current (line 28):**
> The data was comprehensive. The peace was absent.

> **Proposed:**
> The data was comprehensive. But knowing the damage was already done didn't undo it.

**Rationale:** This shifts from "Mint didn't give you peace" (product judgment) to "backward-looking data can't change what already happened" (approach limitation). The critique lands on the methodology, not the tool.

---

### Change 4: Earlier biblical grounding

**Priority:** Medium
**Why:** Biblical Attribution Standard. The article does not mention the Bible, God, or Scripture until line 71 -- 72% of the way through the piece. The recent site update corrected vague "faith" language across articles, but this article has the inverse problem: it is not vague, it is simply absent until the final third. A reader who stops at the "One Number Instead of Forty-Seven" section would encounter the hierarchy as a personal preference rather than a conviction rooted in Scripture.

> **Current (line 47):**
> The answer, for us, was the hierarchy we wrote about in "Give First": give first, save second, pay bills, spend what remains. That's not a budgeting trick. It's a set of values.

> **Proposed:**
> The answer, for us, was the hierarchy we wrote about in "Give First": give first, save second, pay bills, spend what remains. That's not a budgeting trick. It's a biblical principle -- steward what you've been given, starting with what matters most.

**Rationale:** Adding "biblical principle" here gives the reader early attribution without turning the section into a sermon. It connects to the Integrity System's "Attributive, not vague" voice guideline ("When a principle comes from the Bible, say so"). The phrase "steward what you've been given" is natural and brief. This also sets up the "Stewardship, Not Control" section on line 69 so that it deepens a thread rather than introducing one from nowhere.

**Priority:** Low
**Why:** Voice alignment. The closing sentence is good but slightly vague.

> **Current (line 99):**

## Craft Quality

### AI Tells Found

This article is notably clean of AI tells. It reads like human-written prose with genuine editorial voice. A few minor flags:

- **Symmetrical structure (mild):** The three competitor descriptions (lines 25-29) follow a parallel pattern: name the tool, compliment it, explain the limitation. This is the natural structure for a comparison, so it does not read as AI-generated, but adding slight variation in sentence structure across the three would make it feel less formulaic. Not worth a formal change proposal -- the content within each description is varied enough.

- **"Here's the deeper difference" (line 71):** "Here's" as a section opener is a mild filler transition. It is not on the kill-on-sight list but it is a close relative of "Let's dive in." The section heading "Stewardship, Not Control" already signals what is coming. "Here's" adds nothing. However, this is extremely minor and the sentence recovers immediately with strong content.

- **No rule-of-three obsession:** The article avoids the three-item parallel list pattern throughout. The competitor section has three examples, but that is a function of naming three products, not a structural tic. Strong.

- **No hedge phrases:** The article commits to its claims. "The tools are failing at understanding what you actually need" (line 19) is direct. "Control is about grip" (line 73) is direct. No "it's important to note" or "it's worth mentioning" anywhere.

### AI Tells Count: 1 minor (symmetrical competitor descriptions), 1 trivial ("Here's")

### Prose Rhythm: Strong

The article demonstrates genuine sentence-length variation. The opening paragraph (line 13) is a single long, accumulating sentence that mirrors the overwhelm of setting up categories. Lines 17-18 are short, punchy: "You closed the app. You haven't opened it since." The contrast is effective. Lines 61-63 use the shortest possible structure ("If yes, spend freely." / "If no, wait.") to embody the simplicity being described. The "What We're Not Saying" section (lines 79-87) alternates between longer explanatory sentences and short declarative ones. Read aloud, the piece has rhythm and variation.

One note: the "Good Tools, Wrong Question" section (lines 21-33) runs slightly long and dense. Three consecutive competitor descriptions without a breathing sentence between them. A single-line pause or observation between the Mint and Monarch descriptions would help the reader absorb each one.

### Reframes Found

- **"The goal isn't to control your money. It's to steward it well enough that you don't have to think about it constantly." (line 77)** -- The article's core reframe. This works because it takes the reader's assumption (the purpose of a financial tool is control/tracking) and inverts it (the purpose is stewardship that frees you from thinking about money). The reframe is earned through 65 lines of narrative showing why control fails. Strong.

- **"Knowing where your money went doesn't tell you where it should go." (line 41)** -- Secondary reframe. Takes the value proposition of tracking apps (knowledge of spending) and shows its limitation (knowledge without direction). Clean, quotable, effective.

- **"The simplicity isn't laziness." (line 65)** -- Minor reframe that anticipates and inverts the reader's objection to one-number budgeting.

### Engagement: Pulls forward

The opening drops the reader into a specific, recognizable scenario. By line 17 ("You closed the app. You haven't opened it since.") the reader is nodding. Line 19 ("you're not failing at budgeting") provides the first emotional payoff. Each section opens a question that the next section answers: "Good Tools, Wrong Question" sets up the problem, "The Control Problem" diagnoses it, "What We Were Looking For" pivots to the alternative, and so on. The "What We're Not Saying" section is placed perfectly -- right when the reader might be thinking "but I liked YNAB" -- which makes it feel responsive rather than defensive.

The one place engagement dips slightly is the "One Number Instead of Forty-Seven" section (lines 55-67). This ground is covered more thoroughly in Article #2, and a reader who has read that article may feel repetition here. For a reader encountering this article first, the section is necessary. This is an acceptable trade-off.

### Condescension Check: CLEAR

No instances of "simply put," "let me explain," or "let's break this down." The article does not over-explain obvious concepts. The Safe-to-Spend explanation (lines 55-67) is appropriately brief for a concept covered in depth elsewhere. The reader is treated as an intelligent adult who has tried budgeting apps and found them wanting -- the exact posture the Integrity System calls for.

### Guilt Radar: CLEAR

Read as someone $20,000 in debt, living paycheck to paycheck: this article does not shame. Line 19 explicitly reframes failure as a tool problem. Lines 79-87 clear guilt about having used other apps. The article does not prescribe specific amounts or percentages. The only moment that could trigger guilt is the opening scenario (lines 13-17), which describes the reader's experience of seeing red categories -- but this is empathetic narration of what happened to them, not judgment of what they did. The article passes the guilt test.

### Biblical Attribution: ADEQUATE WITH ROOM FOR IMPROVEMENT

The article uses "biblical stewardship" (line 71) and "God provides the rest" (line 75) -- both direct and attributive. No vague "faith" or "ancient wisdom" language appears anywhere. This clears the bar set by the recent site update.

However, these references appear only in the "Stewardship, Not Control" section (lines 69-77), which is the second-to-last section. For 70% of the article, the hierarchy is presented as a personal preference ("the hierarchy we wrote about in 'Give First'") rather than a conviction with a biblical foundation. This is not a violation -- Article #1 handles the full biblical grounding -- but for readers who encounter this article first (likely, given it is SEO-positioned), earlier attribution would strengthen the voice. See Change 4.

### Competitor Respect: STRONG WITH ONE CAUTION

This article now names three competitors by name: YNAB (line 25), Mint (line 27), Monarch (line 29). This is a significant change from the previous version. The overall treatment is respectful:

- YNAB is complimented ("That's a powerful concept") before the limitation is described
- Mint is called "brilliant"
- Monarch is called "beautiful and investment-forward"
- Line 31 explicitly states: "These are well-built tools made by teams who care about financial wellness. We have genuine respect for what they've created."
- Lines 83-85 return to competitor respect: "They've helped millions of families. The teams behind them care about the same things we care about."

The one caution is "the system punishes you" on line 25 (see Change 1). The Craft Guide's Competitor Respect Standard says to critique the approach, not the product. "Punishes" attributes emotional intent to the product's design. Every other competitor reference in the article critiques methodology or approach. This one sentence is the exception.

The Competitor Respect test: "Read every sentence about other apps or approaches as if a loyal, satisfied user of that product is reading it. If they would feel attacked rather than understood, rewrite." A loyal YNAB user reading "the system punishes you" would likely bristle. The same user reading "the recovery work starts to pile up" would likely nod. That is the difference Change 1 addresses.
