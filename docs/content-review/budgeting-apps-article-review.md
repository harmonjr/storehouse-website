### Misses (voice deviations)

- **Line 25 ("The charts are beautiful."):** Minor, but this feels slightly sarcastic in context -- the surrounding sentences are describing what other apps do well, but "beautiful" reads as backhanded when the paragraph's argument is that beauty does not solve the real problem. ## Proposed Changes

### Change 1: Vary the "47 categories" motif

**Priority:** Medium
**Why:** Cross-content consistency (factual consistency dimension). The number 47 appears four times across Articles #2 and #3. A reader encountering both pieces back-to-back will notice the repetition, and it reduces the impact of what is otherwise an effective contrast device.

> **Current (line 27):**
> But here's the problem: knowing where your money went doesn't tell you where it should go. And monitoring 47 categories doesn't reduce financial anxiety -- it multiplies it.

> **Proposed:**
> But here's the problem: knowing where your money went doesn't tell you where it should go. And monitoring dozens of categories doesn't reduce financial anxiety -- it multiplies it.

> **Current (line 63):**
> That's not less rigorous than category tracking. It's differently rigorous. And for the people who've tried 47 categories and given up, it's the difference between a system they abandon and a practice they sustain.

> **Proposed:**
> That's not less rigorous than category tracking. It's rigorous in a different place. And for the people who've tried detailed category budgets and given up, it's the difference between a system they abandon and a practice they sustain.

**Rationale:** Article #2 should own the "47 categories" number since it appears in the title-adjacent metadata. Article #3 can make the same point with "dozens of categories" and "detailed category budgets" without repeating the exact number. The second change also addresses the slightly academic "differently rigorous" phrasing flagged in voice alignment.

---

### Change 2: Soften "The charts are beautiful"

**Priority:** Medium
**Why:** Voice alignment. In context, this reads as slightly backhanded, which contradicts the article's empathetic posture toward other tools.

> **Current (line 25):**
> They're good at what they do. The tracking is accurate. The charts are beautiful. The data is comprehensive.

> **Proposed:**
> They're good at what they do. The tracking is accurate. The data is comprehensive. The insights are real.

**Rationale:** "The charts are beautiful" stands out because it is the only aesthetic compliment in a list of functional ones. It can read as damning with faint praise ("pretty, but useless"). Replacing it with "The insights are real" maintains the generous tone and adds substance.

---

### Change 3: Strengthen the final sentence

**Priority:** Medium
**Why:** Voice alignment and engagement. The closing sentence is good but could be sharper. "The one that starts with what matters" is slightly abstract compared to the concrete, values-grounded language used throughout the piece.

> **Current (line 97):**
> You've tried the apps that track everything. Maybe it's time to try the one that starts with what matters.

> **Proposed:**
> You've tried the apps that track everything. Maybe it's time to try the one that starts with what you value.

**Rationale:** "What you value" echoes the hierarchy-as-values language from Article #1 ("The order you assign your dollars reveals the order you assign your values") and from the "What We Needed" section of this piece (line 41: "Something that started with values, not categories"). It closes the loop more precisely than the vaguer "what matters."
