## Proposed Changes

### Change 1: Strengthen biblical attribution in the hierarchy section

**Why:** Voice Alignment -- "Attributive, not vague" per updated CIS. The current phrasing buries the biblical source in a subordinate clause and uses "the stewardship principle" without saying where the principle comes from.

> **Current (lines 54-55):**
> This is where the Storehouse hierarchy earns its keep -- and where the stewardship principle behind it becomes most practical.

> **Proposed:**
> This is where the Storehouse hierarchy earns its keep -- and where the biblical principle behind it becomes most practical.

And:

> **Current (line 69):**
> But you also directed $300 toward the things that build margin -- giving and saving, the first two priorities the Bible puts ahead of spending. That split doesn't happen without a moment of conscious choice.

> **Proposed:**
> But you also directed $300 toward the things that build margin -- giving and saving, the priorities the Bible puts ahead of spending (Proverbs 3:9). That split doesn't happen without a moment of conscious choice.

The first change substitutes "biblical principle" for "stewardship principle" -- small but direct. The second adds a single Scripture reference. The Craft Guide says one or two citations per article is the sweet spot, and this article currently has zero direct citations.

---

### Change 2: Connect to the Joseph Principle explicitly

**Why:** CIS Section "The Joseph Principle" -- A raise is a "plenty season." This is exactly the Joseph Principle applied: storing during abundance for the lean seasons ahead. The article never uses the term or the seasons language, which is a missed opportunity given the CIS's emphasis on this as a key differentiator alongside Safe-to-Spend and the hierarchy.

> **Current (lines 73-74):**
> Here's the reframe: a raise is a margin opportunity disguised as a spending opportunity. Most people only see the spending.

> **Proposed:**
> Here's the reframe: a raise is a margin opportunity disguised as a spending opportunity. Most people only see the spending. The Joseph Principle -- think in seasons, not just months -- says the plenty season is for building the reserve that carries you through the lean. A raise is a plenty season. What you do with it determines what the next lean season feels like.

This adds two sentences that explicitly invoke the Joseph Principle and the "seasons" language the CIS calls for. It also connects the article's reframe to the broader Storehouse vocabulary.

### Change 3: Tighten the concluding section

**Why:** Craft Quality -- AI Tells (over-qualification, neat conclusion). The "Every Raise Asks the Same Question" section (lines 82-89) qualifies the argument three times in quick succession: "the honest answer might be some of both," "that's fine," "the hierarchy isn't about deprivation," "not perfectly, not every time." This reads like hedging -- the writer is softening the point rather than landing it. The Craft Guide warns about "escape hatches" in every sentence.

> **Current (lines 84-89):**
> Every raise, every bonus, every new income stream presents the same question: does this go to margin or to spending? And the honest answer might be "some of both." That's fine. The hierarchy isn't about deprivation. Upgrading your life as your income grows is one of the genuine rewards of hard work.
>
> The issue isn't the conscious upgrades. It's the unconscious ones -- the spending increases you didn't choose, the margins that eroded without a decision, the raises that vanished into a slightly higher cost of living that doesn't make you any happier than the old one did.
>
> Awareness is the first defense. When you know the pattern, you can see it forming. When you can see it forming, you can choose differently. Not perfectly. Not every time. But enough to keep the margin you've built from quietly slipping away.

> **Proposed (lines 84-89):**
> Every raise, every bonus, every new income stream presents the same question: does this go to margin or to spending? The answer can be "some of both" -- the hierarchy isn't about deprivation. Upgrading your life as your income grows is one of the genuine rewards of hard work.
>
> The issue is the unconscious upgrades -- the spending increases you didn't choose, the margins that eroded without a decision, the raises that vanished into a slightly higher cost of living that doesn't make you any happier than the old one did.
>
> When you know the pattern, you can see it forming. When you can see it, you can choose differently. Not every time. But enough.

This cuts four qualifications down to one ("not every time"), removes filler ("Awareness is the first defense"), and lets the piece end on "But enough" -- shorter, stronger, trusting the reader.

## Craft Quality

### AI Tells Found
- **Over-qualification:** Lines 84-89 hedge the conclusion with four softeners in rapid succession. (See Change 3.)
- **"Here's the [noun]" pattern:** Used twice (lines 35, 73). Acceptable frequency -- no change needed.
- **Mild symmetrical structure:** The $500 raise breakdown (lines 64-68) uses three bullet points, each starting with a dollar amount. This is a natural structure for a financial example and does not read as AI-generated. No change needed.

### Prose Rhythm: Strong
The opening five paragraphs (lines 13-22) are masterful. Each paragraph zooms out one level: first month, second month, six months later, "everywhere and nowhere." The rhythm accelerates as the creep itself accelerates. Lines 17-18 ("The nicer restaurant becomes the regular restaurant...the lunches out aren't a treat -- they're Tuesday") vary sentence length and use repetition purposefully. The article maintains this quality through most sections, though the conclusion (lines 82-89) loses some energy to qualification.

### Reframes Found
- "A raise is a margin opportunity disguised as a spending opportunity." (line 73) -- The strongest reframe in the piece. The reader expected to read about spending restraint. Instead they get a reframe on what a raise is. This works because the reader holds the assumption unconsciously -- raises are for spending more. The inversion is both surprising and obviously true.
- "You didn't blow your money on something foolish. You just let the water level rise one millimeter at a time until the margin was gone." (line 31) -- Good. Takes away the self-blame and replaces it with a structural metaphor. The reader expected to feel guilty; instead they feel understood.

### The "Changed Inside" Test: PASS
The central reframe ("a raise is a margin opportunity") is the moment. The reader arrives at it having walked through the exact experience of watching a raise disappear -- a journey that builds the assumption before inverting it. The conclusion is earned, not stated upfront. The article resists announcing its thesis ("lifestyle creep is bad") and instead walks the reader through the mechanism until they arrive at the insight themselves.

### Engagement: Pulls forward
The opening is one of the best in the foundation series -- "You get a raise. A good one" drops you into a specific experience immediately. Each section builds on the previous: mechanism, why it persists, why it is hard to see, what breaks the cycle, the reframe. The reader moves through the article because each section leaves a question open that the next one answers.

### Condescension Check: Clear
No "simply put," no "let me explain," no over-explanation. "You deserve the nicer dinner, the better shoes" (line 47) treats the reader as an adult whose spending is legitimate.

### Blame & Villainization Check: Clear
No adversarial framing. "Most financial systems" is critiqued structurally (line 57), not by name. The reader's spending is validated ("each one, individually, is completely reasonable") before the pattern is shown. The article blames the mechanism, not the person.
