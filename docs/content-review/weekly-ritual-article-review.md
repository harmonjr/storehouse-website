## Review Summary

This is one of the strongest pieces in the Foundations series -- it takes a potential liability (manual import) and turns it into a selling point through genuine, experience-grounded writing. The previous review's changes (removing the parenting analogy, cutting "universal truth," tightening the auto-sync paragraph) have all been incorporated and the piece is tighter for it. The one significant gap is biblical attribution: the article uses "stewardship" as a load-bearing concept in its closing sections but never names the Bible or God as the source, which conflicts with the "Attributive, not vague" voice guideline and the recent site-wide effort to replace vague faith language with direct attribution. One natural insertion point exists. Two additional craft-level improvements round out the review.

## Proposed Changes

### Change 1: Add biblical attribution to the stewardship section

**Why:** Voice alignment ("Attributive, not vague") and biblical attribution standard. This is the specific gap the recent site-wide update was designed to close. The word "stewardship" appears in the article's most important paragraph (lines 71-79) -- where the weekly ritual connects to the hierarchy -- but the Bible is never named as the source. A reader entering the series at this article would encounter stewardship as a floating concept rather than a grounded one.

The Craft Guide says "one or two citations per article is the sweet spot" and advises weaving attribution naturally, not as proof-texts. One light touch in this section is enough. The article does not need to become a Bible study.

> **Current (lines 71-79):**
> The review isn't just looking at numbers. It's the moment where the hierarchy from "Give First, Save Second, Spend What Remains" stops being a principle and becomes a practice.
>
> You gave this week. You can see it. It's not an idea -- it's a line item.
>
> You saved this week. It's right there, moving from "intention" to "fact."
>
> Your bills are covered. Your Safe-to-Spend reflects reality.
>
> That weekly confirmation is what turns stewardship from something you believe in to something you do. The gap between values and behavior closes in those ten minutes. Not through willpower. Through visibility.

> **Proposed (lines 71-79):**
> The review isn't just looking at numbers. It's the moment where the hierarchy from "Give First, Save Second, Spend What Remains" stops being a principle and becomes a practice.
>
> You gave this week. You can see it. It's not an idea -- it's a line item.
>
> You saved this week. It's right there, moving from "intention" to "fact."
>
> Your bills are covered. Your Safe-to-Spend reflects reality.
>
> That weekly confirmation is what turns biblical stewardship -- the conviction that what you have is God's, and your job is to manage it faithfully -- from something you believe in to something you do. The gap between values and behavior closes in those ten minutes. Not through willpower. Through visibility.

**Priority:** High. This is the primary gap between the article and the current voice standard. The insertion is light (one clause), weaves naturally into the existing sentence, and gives the reader the "why" behind the word "stewardship" without turning the section into a sermon. It also means a reader who enters the series at Article 4 gets the biblical grounding directly rather than needing to have read Article 1 first.

**Alternative (lighter touch):** If the proposed version feels like too much for a practice-focused article, a minimal option: change "stewardship" to "biblical stewardship" on line 79 without the explanatory clause: "That weekly confirmation is what turns biblical stewardship from something you believe in to something you do." This adds one word and signals the source without elaborating. Less informative for a new reader, but lower risk of disrupting the paragraph's rhythm.

### Change 2: Vary sentence structure in "Why Weekly and Not Daily" section

**Why:** Craft quality -- prose rhythm. Lines 49-55 contain a run of medium-length declarative sentences that settle into a predictable cadence. The section has six sentences in a row between 10 and 18 words each. The Craft Guide's prose rhythm section (lines 107-132) warns against this: "If you can predict the rhythm of the next sentence before you read it, the writing is monotonous."

> **Current (lines 51-55):**
> Daily checking turns your finances into a mood ring. The number is up, you feel great. The number is down, you worry. Nothing useful happens in that gap, but the anxiety is constant.
>
> Weekly is different. A week is long enough that the numbers tell a real story. You can see patterns: this was a heavier spending week, or a lighter one. You can connect the numbers to your actual life -- the birthday dinner, the grocery run, the tank of gas. The data has meaning because it has context.

> **Proposed (lines 51-55):**
> Daily checking turns your finances into a mood ring. Up, you feel great. Down, you worry. The number barely moved, but the anxiety is constant.
>
> A week tells a real story. You can see patterns: a heavier spending week, or a lighter one. You can connect the numbers to your actual life -- the birthday dinner, the grocery run, the tank of gas. The data has meaning because it has context.

**Priority:** Medium. The proposed version does three things: (1) shortens the mood-ring sentences to create staccato rhythm that mirrors the daily emotional swings being described, (2) cuts "Weekly is different" as a filler transition -- the section header already establishes the comparison, and (3) trims "A week is long enough that the numbers tell a real story" to "A week tells a real story" for concision. The meaning is unchanged; the rhythm is more varied.

### Change 3: Tighten the closing paragraph to avoid neat-conclusion pattern

**Why:** Craft quality -- the Craft Guide's "Neat Conclusions" warning (lines 63-69). The final paragraph (lines 97-99) wraps up with three sentences that each make a slightly different version of the same point: the ritual is simple, sustainable, and starts with ten minutes. This is warm and encouraging, but it resolves the piece a touch too neatly. The penultimate paragraph (lines 91) already delivered the best closing line in the article ("Coffee, transactions, peace. Ten minutes."). The final section could end with a rougher edge that feels more honest.

> **Current (lines 97-99):**
> The ritual isn't complicated. It's not painful. It's ten minutes of attention that replaces a week of uncertainty. The first one might feel like a chore. The third one will feel like a habit. The tenth one will feel like part of your week you wouldn't skip.
>
> Stewardship isn't a one-time decision. It's a practice you keep. And the practice starts with ten minutes, a cup of coffee, and the willingness to look.

> **Proposed (lines 97-99):**
> The first one might feel like a chore. The third one will feel like a habit. The tenth one will feel like part of your week you wouldn't skip.
>
> Stewardship isn't a one-time decision. It's a practice you keep. And the practice starts with ten minutes, a cup of coffee, and the willingness to look.

**Priority:** Medium. Cutting "The ritual isn't complicated. It's not painful. It's ten minutes of attention that replaces a week of uncertainty." removes a three-sentence preamble that restates what the entire article has already demonstrated. By this point the reader knows it's ten minutes and not painful -- the article earned that understanding across 900 words. The "chore to habit to wouldn't skip" progression is more vivid and does the same work with more forward motion. The closing sentence about stewardship as practice is strong and should remain. (Note: if Change 1 is accepted, the word "stewardship" here will benefit from having been grounded earlier in the piece.)

The article does not use vague language like "ancient wisdom" -- it simply omits attribution entirely. In a practice article, this is less severe than in a principles article, but the word "stewardship" carries the weight of the site's biblical foundation. A reader who enters the series at this article would encounter stewardship as a secular concept. The Craft Guide's biblical attribution standard says one or two citations per article is the sweet spot. This article has zero. Addressed in Change 1.
