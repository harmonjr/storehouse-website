## Proposed Changes

### Change 1: Cross-reference consistency with "Irregular Income and the Hierarchy"

**Why:** Factual Consistency (dimension 4). The existing foundations article "Irregular Income and the Hierarchy" covers freelancer income with a similar baseline concept but frames it differently. That article says "everything above the baseline is windfall" and recommends running windfall through the hierarchy with potentially different percentages. This blog post treats the buffer as a pass-through account that pays a stable "salary." These are compatible approaches but could confuse a reader who encounters both. A cross-reference would resolve this.

> **Current (line 62):**
> Either way, giving doesn't wait for a good month. Even variable income can put giving first. The buffer just makes it less stressful to do so.

> **Proposed:**
> Either way, giving doesn't wait for a good month. Even variable income can put giving first. The buffer just makes it less stressful to do so.
>
> If your income is a mix of steady and irregular -- a base salary plus freelance on the side -- you might find the approach in [Irregular Income and the Hierarchy](/foundations/irregular-income-and-the-hierarchy) a better fit. That piece treats anything above your baseline as windfall and runs each irregular check through the hierarchy individually. The buffer approach works best when all your income is variable and you need a stable floor to stand on.

---

### Change 2: Baseline number discrepancy with cross-referenced article

**Why:** Factual Consistency (dimension 4). The "Irregular Income" foundations article uses a $2,500 or $1,800 baseline and says "look at your last six months of income, find the lowest month." This blog post uses a $900 floor but then adjusts to $1,800 "if that $900 month was an anomaly." The advice to use judgment is fine, but the six-month approach is identical -- these should feel consistent, not contradictory.

> **Current (line 28):**
> If your last six months were $4,200, $1,800, $3,600, $900, $2,800, and $3,100, your baseline is $900. That feels low, so let's be practical: maybe your true floor is closer to $1,800 if that $900 month was an anomaly. Use your judgment.

> **Proposed:**
> If your last six months were $4,200, $1,800, $3,600, $900, $2,800, and $3,100, the lowest month was $900. But one outlier month shouldn't set your floor -- look at the pattern. If $900 was a one-time gap between contracts and $1,800 is closer to your realistic low, use that. The point is to pick a number that holds up in a genuinely slow month, not a worst-case disaster.

The opening four-number sequence (line 8) is punchy and immediately grounding. ### Blame & Villainization Check: Clean
No adversarial framing. Financial advice is critiqued structurally ("how hard it is to find financial advice that starts from that reality," line 10) rather than blaming advisors. No competitors named or attacked.
