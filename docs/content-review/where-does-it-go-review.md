## Proposed Changes

### Change 1: Verify or further qualify the "20-40%" claim

**Priority:** High
**Why:** Factual Consistency (dimension 4) and CIS Financial Accuracy guideline: "Be accurate, be humble about what the app can and can't do, and never let marketing outpace reality." The section header "The 20-40% Problem" presents this range as a named phenomenon. The body text (line 25) now qualifies it with "In our experience," which was the previous review's Option B. This grounding is adequate for the body text. However, the section header still reads as a categorical claim: "The 20-40% Problem" implies an established, documented phenomenon.

Two options to close the remaining gap:

> **Option A -- adjust the header to match the experiential framing:**
>
> **Current (line 24):**
> ## The 20-40% Problem
>
> **Proposed:**
> ## The Perception Gap
>
> This removes the specific percentage from the header (where it reads as a named phenomenon) while preserving it in the body text (where it's qualified with "In our experience"). The header still communicates the same idea but doesn't promise a specific, verifiable range.

> **Option B -- keep the header but add a footnote-style source in the body:**
>
> If research supports this range (Prelec and Loewenstein's work on spending estimation, various consumer surveys), cite the general source in the body text:
>
> **Proposed (line 25):**
> The gap isn't a rounding error. Research on spending estimation consistently finds that most households underestimate their discretionary spending by 20-40%. Groceries, dining, household supplies, personal care, kids' activities -- the areas where spending happens in small, frequent increments.

Either option works. Option A is the lighter touch. Option B is stronger if research exists to support the claim. The current version (body text qualified with "In our experience" but header unqualified) creates a mild tension.

### Change 2: Remove the "Awareness alone doesn't fix this" hedge

**Priority:** Medium
**Why:** AI Tells (Craft Guide watchlist -- abstract over specific). Line 56 opens the section with "Awareness alone doesn't fix this." The sentence that follows already makes the same point more specifically: "You can't maintain an accurate mental model of dozens of variable spending categories across hundreds of transactions per month." The opening line is a hedge -- it pre-qualifies the insight before delivering it. The Craft Guide says: "Say the thing. Trust the reader to recognize its importance."

> **Current (lines 56-59):**
> Awareness alone doesn't fix this. You can't maintain an accurate mental model of dozens of variable spending categories across hundreds of transactions per month. The brain doesn't work that way, and asking it to is how budgeting apps burn people out.
>
> What works is removing the need for the mental model entirely.

> **Proposed:**
> You can't maintain an accurate mental model of dozens of variable spending categories across hundreds of transactions per month. The brain doesn't work that way, and asking it to is how budgeting apps burn people out.
>
> What works is removing the need for the mental model entirely.

Deleting one sentence. The section is tighter without the preamble. The transition from the previous section's problem analysis to this section's solution is cleaner when it opens with the specific constraint rather than the abstract qualifier.
