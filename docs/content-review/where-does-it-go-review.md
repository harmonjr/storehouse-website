## Review Summary

This is a strong Tier 3 Clarity article that names a problem most people feel but can't articulate: the gap between perceived and actual spending. The opening scenario (lines 13-21) is one of the best in the corpus -- concrete, recognizable, and it earns the $620 reveal without overselling it. The "Two Bank Balances" section (lines 45-53) introduces a second dimension of the perception gap that deepens the article beyond a simple "you spend more than you think" piece. The Spending by Category feature introduction (line 61) is well-placed and earns its mention. The primary integrity issue is a mild biblical attribution gap -- the article uses "steward" in its closing line (line 93) and references the founding truth "You can't steward what you can't see" without grounding stewardship in its biblical source. The primary craft issue is the "20-40%" claim (line 25) which needs sourcing or qualification. Four proposed changes, none structural. The single most important issue is the unsourced statistic.

## Proposed Changes

### Change 1: Source or qualify the "20-40%" claim

**Priority:** High
**Why:** Factual Consistency (dimension 4) and Financial Accuracy (CIS guideline). Line 25 states: "For most households, perceived spending runs 20-40% below actual spending across discretionary categories." This is a specific statistical claim presented as fact. The CIS Financial Accuracy section says to flag overpromising and to "be accurate, be humble." If this statistic comes from published research (behavioral economics studies on spending estimation bias are real -- Prelec and Loewenstein, 1998; various consumer surveys), cite the general source. If it's an approximation based on Storehouse user experience, frame it that way.

> **Current (lines 24-25):**
> ## The 20-40% Problem
>
> The gap isn't a rounding error. For most households, perceived spending runs 20-40% below actual spending across discretionary categories.

> **Proposed (option A -- if sourced):**
> ## The 20-40% Problem
>
> The gap isn't a rounding error. Research on spending estimation consistently finds that people underestimate their discretionary spending by 20-40%. Groceries, dining, household supplies, personal care -- the categories where charges arrive in small, frequent amounts.

> **Proposed (option B -- if from experience):**
> ## The 20-40% Problem
>
> The gap isn't a rounding error. In our experience -- and in what we hear from Storehouse users -- most households underestimate their discretionary spending by 20-40%. Groceries, dining, household supplies, personal care -- the categories where charges arrive in small, frequent amounts.

Either option preserves the claim while grounding it. Option A is stronger if research exists. Option B is honest and consistent with Storehouse's kitchen-table voice. The current version presents the number as established fact without attribution, which is a credibility risk for a finance platform.

### Change 2: Ground the closing "steward" in its biblical source

**Priority:** Medium
**Why:** Voice alignment ("Attributive, not vague") and Biblical Attribution Standard. The closing line (line 93) uses the founding truth "You can't steward what you can't see" -- one of Storehouse's most important lines. The word "steward" appears only here, carrying the article's connection to the platform's core concept. But the article never names the Bible, God, or stewardship's biblical origin. For a Tier 3 article, some reliance on Tier 1 grounding is expected. However, this article's closing line is the founding truth itself -- the one that appears in the Integrity System, across multiple articles, and as a site-wide touchstone. Using it without ever naming its source risks the "stewardship as secular concept" pattern identified in reviews of Articles #4, #5, #6, and #9.

The "First Look Is the Hardest" section (lines 77-83) is a natural home for a light attribution touch. It's where the article shifts from mechanism to meaning -- from "here's why the gap exists" to "here's why closing it matters." A brief grounding here would ensure the founding truth in the closing sentence lands with its full weight.

> **Current (lines 80-81):**
> You haven't been fooling yourself. You've been doing what every human brain does -- working from estimates because perfect tracking isn't how life works. The gap doesn't mean you're irresponsible. It means you're normal. And now you're doing something most people never do: looking.

> **Proposed:**
> You haven't been fooling yourself. You've been doing what every human brain does -- working from estimates because perfect tracking isn't how life works. The gap doesn't mean you're irresponsible. It means you're normal. And now you're doing something most people never do: looking. The Bible calls this stewardship -- managing faithfully what you've been given. It starts with seeing clearly.

This adds two sentences that ground the transition to the actionable step. "It starts with seeing clearly" connects to the closing founding truth without restating it verbatim. The attribution is light -- one mention of "the Bible" -- and fits the Craft Guide's "one or two citations per article" guideline. A reader who doesn't share the faith sees a transparent statement about the framework's origin. A reader who does sees the connection between clarity and stewardship made explicit.

> **Current (lines 56-59):**

**AI Tells Count: 1 actionable, 1 borderline noted**

### Prose Rhythm: Strong

The opening is masterful. The first paragraph (lines 13-17) builds a conversational scene, delivers the confident estimate ("$400, maybe $450"), then drops a line break before the reveal: "$620." The single number on its own line is a typographic choice that creates a physical pause -- the reader feels the gap before it's explained. The follow-up paragraph (lines 19-21) varies between long, clause-heavy sentences ("The brain rounds down. It forgets the second trip...") and short punches ("That gap... is where financial margin quietly bleeds out.").

The "Why Your Brain Lies to You" section (lines 33-43) builds through specific examples (birthday present, extra dog food, DoorDash, Target run for paper towels that cost $67) that read like someone's actual month. The "$67 Target run" detail is the kind of specific, slightly absurd number that signals lived experience.

The "Two Bank Balances" section (lines 45-53) is the article's density peak, running longer sentences with more conceptual weight. It's followed by the "What Actually Closes the Gap" section, which opens more practically -- appropriate breathing room after the dense section.

The one flat spot is the "Spending You Can't See" section (lines 67-75), which runs at a fairly uniform medium sentence length. The list of invisible spending types (convenience fees, delivery charges, premium tiers, small upgrades) could benefit from one shorter sentence to break the rhythm.

### Reframes Found
- **"Not because they're bad with money. Not because they're lying. Because nobody walks around with an accurate mental ledger." (line 19)** -- Strong. Takes the reader's fear (I'm bad with money) and redirects it to a universal truth (nobody's mental ledger is accurate). The two "Not because" repetitions create rhetorical rhythm that makes the third beat ("Because nobody...") land harder.
- **"The money didn't disappear. It was always going to leave. You just couldn't see it going." (line 51)** -- Strong. Reframes the "where did my money go?" feeling from loss to visibility. The reader thought money vanished; the article shows it was always committed.
- **"Lost to invisible ones." (line 91)** -- Moderate. The closing reframe: margin isn't lost to bad decisions but to invisible ones. This inverts the self-blame assumption. It works but is less punchy than the earlier reframes because it's nested inside a longer sentence.

**Reframe count: 2 strong, 1 moderate. Meets the minimum.**

### Engagement: Pulls forward

The opening creates immediate recognition. The "$620" reveal builds curiosity ("How did I not know?"). "The 20-40% Problem" quantifies the gap, making it feel systemic rather than personal. "Why Your Brain Lies to You" explains the mechanism through specific, relatable examples. "The Two Bank Balances" introduces a second dimension -- the reader thought the article was only about spending estimation, and now it's about bank balance illusion too. This surprise keeps the reader engaged. "What Actually Closes the Gap" delivers the solution. "The Spending You Can't See" introduces a third dimension (invisible spending). "The First Look" addresses the emotional barrier. The actionable step converts insight to practice. The article builds in layers, with each section adding a new dimension to the same core insight.

### The "Changed Inside" Test

The reader arrives believing: "I have a pretty good handle on what I spend." The reader leaves believing: "My mental model is 20-40% wrong, my bank balance is misleading, and the only way to close the gap is to look at real numbers." The journey from confident estimate to $620 reality is a genuine internal shift. The conclusion is earned through layers of evidence, not stated upfront. Passes.

### Condescension Check: Clean

No "simply put," "let me explain," or "let's break this down." The article treats the reader as an intelligent adult whose brain happens to work the same way everyone's does. The anchoring explanation (line 43) introduces a behavioral science concept without being academic: "There's a name for this in behavioral science: anchoring." One sentence, no jargon parade. The reader learns something without being lectured.

### Blame & Villainization Check: Clean

No adversarial framing. The "enemy" in this article is the brain's own estimation bias, not any person, product, or industry. "Budgeting apps" appear once (line 57: "asking it to is how budgeting apps burn people out") as a generic category critique, not a product attack. No professionals vilified. No blame assigned to anyone's character.
